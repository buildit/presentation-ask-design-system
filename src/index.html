A presentation about design systems

Design systems are a hot topic today. They seem to be the magic solution to all our woes.

But, where did they come from?

What problems do they actually solve?

And, crucially, what do they not solve?

What, the actual fuck, is a design system anyway?

Raise your hand if you've heard of "design systems".

Keep your hand up if you can explain to a friend what they are.

Keep your hand up if you have worked on a design system.

Don't worry - all shall be revealed...

Let's take a step back and look at how we've ended up with design systems...

Does this scenario ring any bells?

You're the developer on a project and the first time you get see actual visual designs, they've already been reviewed and signed off by the client.

For the 5 page microsite you're making, you count 4 distinct button designs, 3 modal overlay styles and 7 different font sizes.

The layout for the "desktop" design demands a different ordering of HTML elements than the corresponding "mobile" design.

All the placeholder text for headings, user names and product features fits perfectly on one line.

Oh! And, it must work on IE8.

Or how about this one?

The 250 page "brand guidelines" PDF that the marketing department sent you, specifies a special typeface for all headings.

You ask for a web font version of it.

You are sent some TTF files.

You explain that you need the web font versions of it.

Several emails later you are sent the same TTF files again.

By the way, none of the TTF files you got are for the weight specified by the brand guidelines for heading text.

Drink a shot if this seems familiar:

You've produced some visual designs and need to share them with the team.

Access to InVision, ZeroHeight and Zeplin is blocked by the company's network.

Access to Dropbox, OneDrive and WeTransfer is also blocked.

You attach your designs to an email and hit send. It bounces back - "maximum attachment size exceeded"

You try to use a USB stick but discover that IT has diabled removable media on your machine.

Someone tells you that there's an MS SharePoint site on the intranet you can use.

You lose the will to live.

...more examples for designer, stakeholder and PM pains

So what have done to counteract all this pain?

Well, we acknowledged the fact that our websites contained recurring elements: Headers, footers, links, buttons, etc.

On the dev side, ever more sophisticated templating languages allowed us to better write the markup for such elements once and re-use them many times over. Likewise, techniques and tools to better scale CSS to large and complex projects arose.

Correspondingly, designers began complementing their full-page mock-ups with styleguide documents. Their tooling improved too. Painstakingly hand-crafted PDF documents gave way to modular vector graphics synchronised and shared via online services.

Wonderful! We had begun breaking down large, complex designs into smaller, more manageable parts. This helped us highlight and eliminate unintentional UI inconsistencies.

End users got more consistent, more usable interfaces to interact with.

Businesses benefitted from less redundancy in the work. Where portions of the UI may have been re-designed, re-developed, re-tested and, when bugs were found, fixed many times over, now they were being made once and re-used many times.

However, we had also unearthed a new set of challenges...

How do we organise and name our UI components?

How far should we break them down?

On the dev side in particular, how do we visualise individual UI components in the browser?

Once again, our methods and tools needed to evolve.

Methodologies like "Atomic Design" gave us a shared mental model and vocabulary to help us organise our libraries of UI components.

Bespoke, manually maintained pages containing "one of everything" were replaced by auto-generated pattern library websites. The likes of Pattern Lab, Fractal, StoryBook and many others allowed us to breathe life into our style guides. They were no longer limited to showing us static mock-ups of how a UI component should look or behave, they could now showcase the actual UI components themselves.

Modular designs and living styleguides make even more sense when you consider all the other aspects our websites need to take into account:

  • Device-agnosticism: Your components can have responsive layouts and respond to various forms of input.
  • Accessibility: Visual aspects like font sizes and colour contrast. Technical implementations that play nice with assistive technologies.
  • Robustness: Layouts and behaviours that can cope with highly variable content. Use of progressive enhancement to ensure that users stuck on archaic browsers, or simply with iffy mobile reception, can access content and complete tasks.
  • The list goes on...

We can bake a lot of that into the design and implementation of the components themselves. Our living styleguides are key enablers for doing so as they provide us with instant and real world feedback as we do so.

Unsurprisingly, crafting well organised, beautiful, living styleguides is rapidly becoming the de-facto way we approach much of our UI work.

However, our journey does not end there! Once you have a living styleguide, a whole slew of questions inevitably arise...

How do we share UI code between our style guide and our website?

How do we continually keep both up-to-date and in sync?

What happens when we need to support more than a single website?

How do we cope with multiple CMSes and front-end frameworks?

What happens when there is a re-design?

Do we start over again, beginning with static, visual designs?

Or, can we evolve our UI components directly in the code?

With so many questions and challenges, you could be forgiving for throwing your hands up in despair and running back to PhotoShop's bosom.

That's why it's so important to remember how we got here. Living style guides are better than what went before. They are the culmination of many solutions to many problems.

They're just not an excuse to sit still. Rather, they are your gateway drug into something far bigger and more exciting.

You have unwittingly begun work on your design system!

People struggle to explain design systems succinctly because they are not one thing.

Depending on your point of view, they might be described the effort to scale up your living style guide to serve an entire organisation.

From a business person's perspective, they could be considered the means by which an organisation gets more efficient at delivering consistent, high-quality UIs.

The marketing team might simply think of them as the replacement for their old brand guidelines PDF.

Maybe they are the never-ending struggle to discover the perfect way to make interactive products, that we are all doomed to persue!

The one thing everyone does agree on is that design systems must encompass code, designs, documentation, tools and people. Furthermore, they are never "done" - in order to continuously deliver value, they must themselves be maintained continuously.

Perhaps "design eco-system" would have been a more appropriate name!

So, what can we all do to grow our design systems and keep them healthy?

And so, my fellow webmasters: ask not what your design system can do for you
— ask what you can do for your design system.
